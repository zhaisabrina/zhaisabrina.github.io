CareConnect

Visual/UX Design, User testing, User research
Problem

There are many medical misconceptions surrounding ailments, especially when it comes down to the most common illnesses. As the internet and information from it becomes more and more prevalent, self-diagnosis can be conducted inaccurately or simply give too much information to the user to digest understandably.

Solution

CareConnect is meant to make that confusing and sometimes daunting experience easier and friendlier, so that users take the time to truly understand what steps they need to take to get better.

My role & contribution

I worked with Dr. Li to create this app. I was in charge of the user tests, user interface, and user flow. I consuled with the doctors often to make sure that everything is medically accurate.














Competitive Analysis

Taking a closer look at Waze, it uses five different icons, with the background color of the icon’s circle delineating the severity of the situation. Waze also highlights the different types of roads in different colors, which, compared to Google Maps’ more subdued/pastel color palette, allows the users to clearly see what types of roads these are.











Ideation

A glance into my sketchbook! While consulting with Dr. Li, I talked to her about her ideas for the application and went through the different things we could do with this brainchild. Coupled with our competitive analysis, we created these following ideas.











User Journey

After analyzing WebMD and several other diagnosis apps, we began to look at the holes I saw in their user flow and began to map out the user journey of CareConnect. Through this we looked to incorporate less daunting and friendlier interactions into an app that organized and compartmentalized the medical information that we felt were harder to digest.











Paper Prototype

After we hashed out the user journey, I creaed these low fidelity wireframes to discuss with Dr. Li. Here's the feedback she had for me:

She wanted some way of indicating when it was time to go see a doctor. We discussed the necessity for this; I had thought it was a little roundabout to use an app to diagnosis yourself, only for the solution to be "Go see a doctor!" instead of directly seeing/calling a doctor. She agreed, and we drafted the user flow to include "warning symptoms" where it simply indicates the possibility that this may lead to something more serious that our app can handle down the road.

I felt like having this in the symptoms (rather than in the diagnosis - so after everything was over), had a greater chance of preventing users from missing their key warning symptoms (one of the key painpoints Dr. Li wanted to hit on).









Low Fidelity Prototype

After the sketches were complete, I began to work on the low fidelity mockups on Sketch. I wanted to make sure that the user experience / user journey worked first, before I began to implement more details such as images and colors.















User Testing

After completing the low-fidelity mockups, I conducted a user test before moving on to higher fidelity wireframes. I wanted to evaluate if the flow of the symptom checker made sense, and was intuitive, because it slightly differed from other symptom checkers (from my competitive analysis).

Main feedback:

  • Unclear what “warning symptom” and “leading symptom” are
  • Wants to know possible causes of an illness after a diagnosis
  • For Possible Diagnosis, hierarchy of information is unclear
  • Adding details UI is a little confusing, can get messy really quickly
  • Curious as to where confidence levels come from (some sort of explanation needed)
  • Recovery Time UI can be fine for simple diagnosis, but for more complicated datasets, may be too fuzzy





Mid Fidelity Wireframes
